Working with Cameras

It is recommended that we use the Physical camera when working with AMD FireRender.  To create a Physical camera you can place a camera in the scene by hand or you can create a Physical camera from your current perspective view.

To place a camera in your scene by hand change the [Perspective] view to the [Top] view.  Click the camera tab, click the Physical camera button, click and hold in the scene to place the camera, then drag and release to set the target for the camera.  For our purposes, using the car scene set up in the previous chapters, we'll place the target in the middle of the car.

Change the [Top] view back to [Perspective] and rotate the view so we can see the car and the camera.  Right click on the camera and select Move.  Move the camera up and across so you have a nice shot of the car.  You will see that the camera automatically rotates to point at the target set in the middle of the car.

If you right click the [Perspective] you will see there is an option to select Cameras -> PhysCamera001.  This will allow you to change the view to the camera [PhysCamera001].  You may need to swap back and forth between the 2 views to get a good positioning on the car. If you have the ActiveShade renderer open you can also see the results.

An alternative way to position a Physical camera is to move you current [Perspective] view to the desired view you want for the camera by holding down the middle mouse button to pan and pressing the Alt key, while still holding the middle mouse button, to rotate .  Once you have the view you want you can then click the Create -> Cameras -> Create Physical Camera From View in the main menu or press Ctl + C.

You will notice that the [Perspective] view has automatically been changed to the [PhysCamera001] view.

There are a large number of setting on the Physical camera related to photography that can change the result of the image being taken.  We will not cover these as they are well documented elsewhere and on the Internet.  There are some settings however within the Render -> Render Setup -> FireRender Settings that can quickly help us add more realism to our image.

Close the ActiveShade render window and open the Render -> Render Setup -> FireRender Settings window.  Open the ActiveShade window again as we sometimes need to close and open it again to see the effects of our setting changes. If you scroll down to the FireRender Settings -> Tone Mapping and press the Linea (Photographic Exposure) you will notice a dramatic change to the resulting image.  

Change the Exposure to 0.1 and the F Stop to 5.6.  The Tone Mapping is a post render image process so does not require the renderer to re-render the image.   The resulting image now is much brighter and the highlights much clearer.  

We can also add Depth of Field (DOF) to the image.  This allows the camera to focus on the Target point allowing areas of the image that are either closer or further away to be blurred as if we are using a real lens.

If you enable DOF within the FireRender Settings you will affect all the camera including the [Perspective] view.  This could be confusing as you may want your perspective view to be clean without the DOF effect so we will apply the DOF to the PysCamera001 only.

To achieve this we need to also change the Render Mode to Production Render Mode.  Open the Render -> Render Setup and change the Target to Production Rendering Mode.  With this new mode you will also need to reinstate the values of the Tone Mapping Linear Exposure to 0.1 and F Stop to 5.6 as each mode has it's own settings values.

Change the view [Perspective] and move the view so you can see the camera and the car.  Select the camera and check the Lens -> Enable Depth of Field check box.  You will notice that there are 2 new rectangles on the camera's view guide.  These show the area where the camera will be in focus.  Change the Target Distance value so that the center rectangle and Target object lines up with the car's headlights.  Change the Camera Aperture to 64.

Change the [Perspective] back to the [PhysCamera001] and Render.  You will see that the front of the car is in focus with the rear of the car slightly out of focus.
